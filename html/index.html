Spexy

Spexy is a module which translates a dialect of Lisp into Python. It can be used as an encoding to transparently have Python understand our particular subset of Lisp. Spexy does not pollute the generated output with helper functions or methods, and any output generated by Spexy does not depend upon Spexy.

What and Why

I've heard my grandmother say "there's nothing new under the sun," more than a few times. There's already a Scheme interpreter written in Python, and there's already an S-Expression Python encoding. However, neither of these work like Spexy.

Spexy can do interesting things like give you a let form, and a setf to go with it. It gives you a lambda form with a body of more than one expression (because there's also a progn form). It doesn't take away dotted accessors to Python objects. It has variadic forms of and,or,+,- and a way to pass those forms as first-class functions

Inspiration

The short explanation is that I was just messing around with lambda one day, and I realized that I could make a let form. This all exploded out of that.

The long explanation involves me discovering the church of lambda (in python). I thought it was super keen, but didn't understand it at first. After staring at it for about a day, the meaning hit me like a ton of bricks, and I felt euphoric and danced around a little. After the bouncing and giggling wore off, I decided to try to implement it myself, from scratch. And when I'd done that, I decided to try and implement some additional things (which almost gave me a nosebleed, but I perservered) using only unary lambdas. The very next day, casual web-browsing led me to a fellow who hacked out a curly-braces encoding for Python on a whim, and the two ideas smashed together with a resounding squish. From that tangled, giddy mental landscape arose the realization that with a few lambas, I could add mutable closures!

And why in the world would I think about mutable closures? Because not long before all of this, I'd been messing with another project, which I call Brine. Brine permits the pickling of Python's code and cells, and on top of that will allow you to pickle anonymous and bound functions.

I spent time on this because it makes me happy, and because I simply can't play World of Warcraft all of the time.
